Credit Bureaus and Your ITIN: Experian, Equifax & TransUnion

The three major U.S. credit bureaus each maintain their own database, and they work independently of each other. Here's how each one handles ITIN holders, and what to do if something looks wrong.

Quick Answer

Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion all create credit files for ITIN holders. Each bureau operates independently, your data and score can differ across all three. ITIN holders are somewhat more likely to encounter "mixed file" errors (another person's data on your report). Pull reports from all three at AnnualCreditReport.com and dispute any inaccuracies immediately.

What does a credit bureau actually do?

A credit bureau (also called a credit reporting agency) collects data on your borrowing and repayment behavior from lenders, credit card issuers, and other creditors, then compiles that data into a credit report. FICO and other scoring companies pull your report from a bureau and run it through a scoring algorithm to produce your credit score. Bureaus do not make lending decisions, they collect and report data. The three bureaus are private companies and operate independently. They share no data with each other automatically; a creditor who reports your account to Experian does not automatically report it to Equifax or TransUnion. This is why your credit file and score can look different across all three.

How do you check your TransUnion credit report with an ITIN?

TransUnion builds a credit file for ITIN holders the same way it does for anyone else, once a creditor reports account activity under your ITIN. To pull it, request your free report through AnnualCreditReport.com or directly at transunion.com. The online identity check sometimes fails for ITIN holders with a short U.S. address history, because the verification questions draw on public records that may still be thin. If that happens, request the report by mail or phone instead of forcing the online path. Disputes go through transunion.com/credit-disputes, and TransUnion has to investigate within 30 days. TransUnion data also feeds Credit Karma and a large share of lender pulls, so a clean, accurate TransUnion file matters more than its single-bureau share suggests. If TransUnion returns "no file found," it means no creditor has reported to them under your ITIN yet, open one account that reports to TransUnion and a file usually starts within a couple of months.

How do you check your Experian credit report with an ITIN?

Experian is often the easiest of the three for ITIN holders to reach directly. Pull your report free through AnnualCreditReport.com, or create a free account at experian.com using your ITIN where the form asks for a Social Security Number. Experian also runs Experian Boost, which lets you add on-time utility, rent, phone, and streaming payments to your file from a linked bank account, a rare way for a thin ITIN file to pick up positive history that would otherwise never report. Disputes are handled at experian.com/disputes, with the standard 30-day investigation window. Because Experian is the bureau many consumer credit-building products report to first, it is frequently where an ITIN file appears earliest. If you are just starting out, check Experian before assuming you have no credit at all, a secured card or credit-builder loan that reports here can show up months before the other two bureaus catch up.

How do you check your Equifax credit report with an ITIN?

Equifax maintains ITIN credit files and offers both online and offline access. The free route is AnnualCreditReport.com or a myEquifax account at equifax.com, entering your ITIN in the SSN field. If online verification fails, Equifax accepts requests by phone at 1-800-685-1111 or by mail, which is often the more reliable path for ITIN holders whose public-record footprint is limited. Disputes run through equifax.com/personal/disputes, and like the other bureaus Equifax must finish its investigation within 30 days. Equifax is frequently the bureau where a "no file found" result lingers longest, because some creditors report to it last, so an empty Equifax file while Experian already shows data is normal, not a mistake. The fix is the same everywhere: open at least one account that reports to Equifax specifically, then give it a billing cycle or two to appear. Re-pull after 60 days to confirm the file has opened.

Can you use Credit Karma with an ITIN?

This is the honest catch most ITIN guides skip. Credit Karma is free and shows TransUnion and Equifax data, but its signup has historically required a Social Security Number, and many ITIN holders find they cannot create an account with an ITIN alone. If Credit Karma rejects your ITIN, that is a platform limitation, not a sign that you have no credit, your file still exists at the bureaus themselves. The reliable alternatives are pulling directly from AnnualCreditReport.com (free, ITIN accepted, no SSN requirement) or opening free accounts at experian.com and equifax.com, which do accept ITINs. ITIN holders who later receive an SSN, or who hold both, can usually access Credit Karma once an SSN is on file. Until then, treat Credit Karma as optional convenience rather than the foundation of your monitoring. The bureaus and AnnualCreditReport give you the same underlying data without the signup wall.

How do you use AnnualCreditReport.com with an ITIN?

AnnualCreditReport.com is the only federally authorized free-report site, set up by the three bureaus under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, and it accepts ITINs. Since 2023 you can pull all three reports free every week, not just once a year, which makes it the cheapest way to watch a growing ITIN file. Enter your ITIN where the form asks for a Social Security Number, then your name, current address, and date of birth. The site runs an identity quiz based on public records, and ITIN holders with a short U.S. history sometimes fail it, in which case you can request reports by mail using the printable request form. Because it covers all three bureaus in one place, this is the right starting point before paying for any monitoring service. Pull all three, compare them side by side, and note which creditors report to which bureau so you know where your file is strongest.

How do bureaus match a credit file to your ITIN?

When a lender reports your account activity to a bureau, they send a data record that includes your name, address, date of birth, and tax identification number (SSN or ITIN). The bureau's matching algorithm uses these data points, weighted differently by each company, to link the record to an existing file or create a new one. For SSN holders, the SSN is a nearly unique identifier that drives reliable matching. For ITIN holders, matching relies more heavily on the combination of name, address, and date of birth, which creates a higher chance of overlap with other people. If your address matches another person's address and your names are similar, there's a real risk of a mixed file, accounts from a different person appearing on your report.

What is a mixed file and how do you fix it?

A mixed file occurs when the bureau's system links account data from two different people because their identifying information is similar enough to trigger a false match. You might see accounts you never opened, addresses you've never lived at, or an employer you've never worked for. Mixed files can cause your score to drop based on another person's delinquencies, or cause you to be denied credit due to that person's negative history. If you find a mixed file: dispute every incorrect item online at each bureau, clearly state that the account belongs to a different person, and provide a copy of your ITIN letter and a government-issued photo ID. The bureau must investigate within 30 days and correct verified errors. Requesting a fraud alert while the investigation is open is also advisable.

A step-by-step plan to dispute a mixed-file error

The FTC's landmark study of credit reports found roughly 1 in 5 consumers had a verified error on at least one report, and mixed-file errors hit thin-file and ITIN consumers hardest because matching leans on name and address. If you spot an account that isn't yours, work this exact sequence rather than firing off a single online dispute:

  1. Document before you dispute. Save a dated PDF or screenshot of the report showing the wrong account, you want a record of what was there before it (hopefully) disappears.
  2. Dispute at the bureau that shows the error, in writing. A written dispute (online portal upload or certified mail) creates a paper trail an over-the-phone dispute does not. State plainly: "This account does not belong to me; it appears to be a mixed file."
  3. Attach proof of identity. Include your ITIN assignment letter and a government photo ID so the bureau can distinguish you from the person whose data was merged in.
  4. Dispute with the furnisher too. Contact the lender that reported the account (the "furnisher") in parallel. Under the FCRA they have their own obligation to investigate, and fixing it at the source stops it from reappearing.
  5. Hold them to the 30-day clock. The bureau must investigate and respond within 30 days and remove anything it cannot verify. If they miss the deadline or "verify" an account that clearly isn't yours, escalate by filing a complaint with the CFPB.
  6. Re-pull all three reports afterward. A mixed file often appears on more than one bureau. Confirm the correction landed everywhere, not just where you disputed.

How to pull your report from all three bureaus

The fastest approach is visiting AnnualCreditReport.com, the officially authorized free-report portal operated by the three bureaus under the Fair Credit Reporting Act. Select all three bureaus, enter your ITIN where the form asks for a Social Security Number, and provide your name, address, and date of birth. You'll receive a link to each bureau's report. If identity verification fails on the site (which occasionally happens for ITIN holders with limited U.S. address history), you can request a report by phone or mail directly from each bureau. Review all three reports side by side, differences between them reflect which creditors report to which bureau, and errors are often present on only one report.

What if your file shows "no credit history"?

If a bureau returns no file or no scoreable history, it means no creditor has reported any account activity associated with your ITIN to that bureau yet. This isn't an error, it's a starting state. The solution is opening at least one account that reports to that specific bureau. Secured cards and credit-builder loans that explicitly report to all three bureaus are the most efficient way to build a file at every bureau simultaneously. Once you have six months of reported history on at least one account, a FICO score can be generated. For step-by-step instructions, see our building credit history guide. Common questions

Do all three credit bureaus accept ITINs?

Yes. Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion all create credit files for ITIN holders. When a lender or creditor reports your account activity with your ITIN as the tax ID, the bureau matches it to your file or creates one. The process works the same as for SSN holders, though identity verification steps may differ slightly.

What is a mixed credit file and how does it happen with an ITIN?

A mixed file occurs when account information belonging to a different person ends up on your credit report. ITIN holders face a somewhat higher risk because bureau matching historically relied heavily on SSNs; files matched primarily on name, address, and date of birth can overlap when those details are similar. If you see accounts you don't recognize, dispute them immediately.

How do I get a free copy of my credit report?

Visit AnnualCreditReport.com, the only federally authorized free-report site. You're entitled to one free report per year from each of the three bureaus. Enter your ITIN where the form asks for a Social Security Number. The system accepts both.

Does each bureau have a separate credit score?

Each bureau has its own file on you, and FICO (and VantageScore) calculate a score from each file independently. Your Experian score, Equifax score, and TransUnion score can differ if the data in each file differs. Lenders often check only one bureau; which one varies by lender and product.

What if Experian has my file but Equifax says "no file found"?

This is common when some of your creditors only report to certain bureaus. You may need to open accounts that report to all three, or contact the bureau with no file directly to initiate a file. Secured cards and credit-builder loans that report to all three bureaus resolve this most efficiently.

Can I use Credit Karma with an ITIN?

Often no, at least not with an ITIN alone. Credit Karma is free and shows TransUnion and Equifax data, but its signup has historically required a Social Security Number, and many ITIN holders cannot create an account without one. A rejection is a platform limitation, not a sign you have no credit. Pull your reports free at AnnualCreditReport.com or open free accounts at experian.com and equifax.com, all of which accept ITINs, to see the same underlying data.
