ITIN Mortgage Loans: How to Buy a Home Without an SSN (2026)

By Lucía Morales · Published June 12, 2026

Quick Answer

Yes — ITIN holders can get a mortgage through specialized Non-QM lenders, portfolio banks, and community credit unions. Most programs require a 620+ credit score, a 10–20% down payment, two years of tax returns filed with your ITIN, and documented income. No Social Security Number is needed.

Owning a home in the United States without a Social Security Number is not a loophole or a workaround — it is a legitimate, well-established path taken by hundreds of thousands of immigrant families every year. If you have an ITIN and are wondering whether homeownership is within reach, the short answer is yes. The longer answer involves understanding the right loan type, what lenders actually look for, and how your credit score affects every term you’ll be offered.

Wait — I thought you needed an SSN to get a mortgage?

A question we hear often: This is the most common misconception we encounter in this space. Conventional mortgages backed by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac do require a Social Security Number, and FHA, VA, and USDA loans follow similar rules. But there is an entire category of lending outside those government-backed programs.

Most traditional loans, like those backed by Fannie Mae or FHA, have strict requirements around residency status and SSN. Because ITIN holders don’t fit those “qualified mortgage” boxes, ITIN loans fall under a category called Non-QM loans. Non-QM simply means non-qualified mortgage — lenders who write their own underwriting guidelines rather than following federal agency rules.

ITIN mortgages are typically portfolio loans, meaning the lender keeps them on their books rather than selling them on the secondary market. This gives lenders flexibility to set their own underwriting guidelines. That flexibility is exactly what makes ITIN homeownership possible — and it is 100% legal.

Who qualifies for an ITIN home loan?

ITIN mortgage loans allow individuals without a Social Security Number to qualify for a home loan using an IRS-issued Individual Taxpayer Identification Number instead, if they can meet the income, documentation, and down payment requirements. The key phrase there is if they can meet — lenders are not handing these out freely, but the bar is knowable and reachable with preparation.

Here is what the typical ITIN mortgage application requires:

  • Valid ITIN — the official IRS letter or card assigning your number
  • Two years of U.S. tax returns filed using that ITIN
  • Proof of income — pay stubs for W-2 employees, or 12–24 months of bank statements for self-employed borrowers
  • Government-issued photo ID — passport, consular ID, or state-issued ID
  • Down payment funds — sourced and documented in your bank account
  • Credit history — either a traditional score or alternative documentation (more on this below)

You will need to provide proof of two years of consistent employment, whether a W-2 job or self-employment in your own business. Your lender will want to see the last two years of tax returns. Self-employed ITIN holders often use bank statement programs as an alternative to tax returns.

What credit score do I actually need?

This one comes up a lot: The credit score question is where most ITIN applicants feel the most uncertainty — especially those who are still in the early stages of building their U.S. credit file.

Most ITIN mortgage programs require a minimum credit score of around 620. Some lenders accept alternative credit documentation for borrowers without traditional credit histories. Higher credit scores typically qualify for lower down payments. Some lenders may consider scores as low as 580, but typically only when supported by compensating factors such as larger down payments or additional cash reserves.

Your score doesn’t just determine approval — it directly shapes your rate and your down payment. Scores of 680 or higher usually qualify for more competitive ITIN mortgage rates. Borrowers with scores above 720 often gain access to the lowest available rates and minimum down payment options offered within ITIN programs. If you’re currently below 620, the most productive move is to spend 6–12 months deliberately building your credit before applying. Our guide on how to build credit with an ITIN number covers the fastest strategies to get there.

And if you have no credit score yet? Alternative credit is commonly used when a borrower does not have an established credit score. Instead of relying on a credit bureau report, lenders review documented payment history on recurring obligations. Most ITIN lenders look for at least 12 months of on-time payments across three to four accounts.

The down payment is usually the biggest planning hurdle, and the numbers vary more than most guides admit. Here’s an honest breakdown:

Because these are considered “higher risk” by the industry, you can expect an interest rate that is roughly 1% to 3% higher than the standard market rate for conventional loans. That premium is real, and it underscores why building a stronger ITIN credit score before you apply can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of a 30-year loan.

Borrowers with credit scores above 720 often receive rates that are 0.5% to 1% lower than those offered to borrowers in the 640 to 679 range. Over the life of a 30-year mortgage, that difference can translate into tens of thousands of dollars in additional interest.

What kind of lender should I look for?

Readers frequently ask: this question because the landscape looks nothing like what most people expect from a mortgage search.

ITIN loans are primarily offered by non-QM specialty lenders, portfolio banks, credit unions, and mortgage brokers who work with wholesale lenders. Major national banks typically don’t offer ITIN programs, making it important to seek out specialized ITIN mortgage lenders.

Some states have more established ITIN lending markets than others, particularly those with larger immigrant populations. California, Texas, Florida, New York, and Illinois tend to offer a broader range of ITIN mortgage options. If you live outside those states, a mortgage broker with Non-QM experience is often your best route — they can access wholesale lenders nationwide that a local bank branch simply cannot.

Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) are another often-overlooked option. These are nonprofit lenders certified by the U.S. Treasury that focus specifically on underserved and immigrant communities, and many actively work with ITIN borrowers on homeownership programs.

One important caution: because rates for ITIN loans can be higher, shopping around is not just optional — it’s a financial necessity. Don’t settle for the first offer you receive. Getting two or three competing loan estimates before committing can make a meaningful difference.

What if I have no U.S. credit history at all — can I still qualify?

Yes, with the right preparation. Since many applicants lack a standard FICO score, lenders often rely on alternative credit, like 12 months of canceled rent checks or utility bills. For income verification, 12 to 24 months of personal or business bank statements are frequently used instead of traditional W-2s.

The practical implication: if you are renting right now, document everything. Save every canceled check, every electronic payment receipt, and every utility confirmation. This paper trail becomes your credit file when you do not yet have a formal score. You can also accelerate the process of building a traditional FICO score by opening a secured credit card or credit builder loan — products specifically designed for ITIN holders that report to all three bureaus.

According to Experian’s February 2026 white paper, 76.9% of ITIN holders remained current on trades after 12 months, a rate 15% higher than SSN consumers — which is exactly why lenders who understand this population are increasingly willing to work with alternative documentation. The data supports the trust.

How does my ITIN credit history actually look to a mortgage lender?

Once you have accounts reporting to the bureaus, your credit file under your ITIN functions exactly like any other. ITIN credit scores function identically to SSN-based FICO and VantageScore models. A lender pulling your credit will see the same three-digit score, the same payment history, and the same utilization ratios — the only difference is the identifying number attached to the file.

What is different is that your file may be thinner, especially early on. Not all three bureaus may track you initially. Some may start after your first credit account. Apply for products that report to all three bureaus to build a complete credit file faster. A mortgage lender will typically pull a tri-merge report (all three bureaus) and use the middle score, so gaps in any bureau’s data can create problems. That’s another reason to check how to get your credit report with an ITIN well before you begin the mortgage process.

What are the most common reasons ITIN mortgage applications get denied?

A question we hear often: Understanding why applications fail is just as useful as knowing what to submit.

Most ITIN loan denials happen for a small number of predictable reasons. Inconsistent income — large gaps in employment or irregular bank deposits that cannot be clearly explained. Missing ITIN tax filings — failure to file U.S. tax returns using the ITIN in recent years, or gaps in filing history. No verifiable credit history — lack of a traditional credit score combined with insufficient alternative credit documentation. Insufficient down payment — not having enough funds to cover the minimum down payment, closing costs, and required reserves.

All four of these are preventable with time and planning. If your ITIN tax filing history has gaps, address them with a qualified tax professional before you apply. If your income is irregular, a bank statement loan program may be a better fit than a W-2-based application. And if your down payment is close but not quite there, waiting an additional 6–12 months to build reserves often gets you better terms anyway.

FAQs

What credit score do I need for an ITIN mortgage? Most ITIN mortgage programs require a minimum between 620 and 660. Some lenders accept alternative credit documentation for borrowers without any traditional score. Scores above 680 unlock better rates; 720+ gives you the widest access to the most competitive ITIN programs.

How much down payment is required for an ITIN home loan? Expect 10–20% in most cases. Some specialty programs advertise as low as 3.5% down, but 15–20% is the realistic standard. A stronger credit score helps reduce the required down payment.

Can I get an ITIN mortgage if I have no U.S. credit history? Yes. Lenders will typically require at least 12 months of documented on-time payments across three or four recurring accounts — rent, utilities, or phone bills — as alternative credit.

Which lenders offer ITIN mortgages? Non-QM specialty lenders, portfolio banks, community credit unions, CDFIs, and mortgage brokers working with wholesale lenders. Major national banks generally do not participate in ITIN programs.

Is getting an ITIN mortgage legal? Completely legal. ITIN mortgages are regulated products offered by licensed lenders. There is no law barring non-citizens or undocumented residents from purchasing U.S. property.

What documents do I need? Your ITIN letter or card, two years of tax returns filed under that ITIN, proof of income (pay stubs, W-2s, or 12–24 months of bank statements), government-issued photo ID, bank statements, and — if you lack a credit score — 12+ months of documented rent and utility payment history.

Common questions

What credit score do I need for an ITIN mortgage?

Most ITIN mortgage programs require a minimum credit score between 620 and 660. Some lenders will go as low as 580 with compensating factors like a larger down payment. Borrowers with scores of 680 or higher qualify for more competitive rates, and those above 720 typically access the best terms available within ITIN programs.

How much down payment is required for an ITIN home loan?

Down payment requirements typically fall between 10% and 20% of the purchase price, depending on your credit profile and lender. Some specialty programs advertise as low as 3.5% down, but 15–20% is the most common real-world requirement. A stronger credit score can help lower the required down payment.

Can I get an ITIN mortgage if I have no U.S. credit history?

Yes. If you have no traditional credit score, many ITIN lenders will accept alternative credit documentation — such as 12 months of on-time rent payments, utility bills, or phone bills — across at least three to four accounts. This is called alternative credit and is a standard option in Non-QM underwriting.

Which banks or lenders offer ITIN mortgages?

ITIN mortgages are primarily offered by Non-QM specialty lenders, portfolio banks, community credit unions, and mortgage brokers who work with wholesale lenders. Major national banks like Chase or Wells Fargo generally do not offer ITIN programs. States with large immigrant populations — California, Texas, Florida, New York, and Illinois — tend to have the widest selection of ITIN-friendly lenders.

Is getting an ITIN mortgage legal?

Absolutely. ITIN mortgage loans are completely legal, regulated products. There is no law preventing non-citizens or undocumented residents from purchasing property in the United States. ITIN mortgages are offered by licensed lenders and simply use your IRS-issued ITIN in place of a Social Security Number for identification and tax purposes.

What documents do I need to apply for an ITIN mortgage?

You will typically need: your valid ITIN (IRS letter or card), two years of tax returns filed under that ITIN, proof of income (pay stubs, W-2s, or 12–24 months of bank statements for self-employed borrowers), a government-issued photo ID, and recent bank statements. If you lack a credit score, bring 12+ months of documented rent and utility payment history.
