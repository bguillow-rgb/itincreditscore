Rent Reporting Services That Work With an ITIN (2026)

By Editorial Team · Credit Scores & Building · Published July 24, 2026

Advertiser disclosure:ITIN Credit Score is an independent resource. Some links on this page are from partners who compensate us, which may affect which products we feature and where. This does not change our editorial guidance and is never a cost to you. See our full disclosure.

Quick Answer

ITIN holders can use rent reporting services like Boom, Rental Kharma, and RentReporters to add monthly rent payments to Equifax, TransUnion, or all three bureaus. Most services accept an ITIN in place of an SSN. Consistent on-time payments can raise your score by 20-40+ points within the first 30-60 days of reporting.

Rent is most people’s largest monthly bill, yet for most ITIN holders it has always been invisible to the credit bureaus. That is changing. Rent reporting platforms now submit your monthly rent to one or more of the three major bureaus so the payment shows up as a tradeline on your credit report. If you have a thin credit file and you pay rent on time, this is one of the fastest, lowest-cost moves available to you.

Does paying rent actually show up on a credit report with an ITIN?

A question we hear often: not automatically, but yes, it can, with the right setup.

The credit bureaus don’t pick up rental payments on their own. They gather data from furnishers like credit card companies and lenders, not from landlords. When you enroll in a rent reporting service, that platform becomes the furnisher for your rental information.

You need either a landlord who agrees to share payment data or a service that supports landlord-free reporting, and you need a valid SSN or ITIN for bureau matching. Most major platforms explicitly accept an ITIN. During enrollment, you enter your ITIN where the form asks for a tax ID, and the service uses it to match your payments to your existing bureau file.

One practical detail worth knowing: the most widely used FICO version, FICO 8, does not incorporate rent payments. Many lenders are moving to FICO 9 or FICO 10, both of which do. VantageScore 3.0 and 4.0 also factor in rental history. So the score impact you see will depend partly on which model your future lender uses when they pull your report.

Boom reports to all three bureaus for about $3 per month, requires no landlord participation, and uses positive-only reporting, meaning a missed payment won’t be submitted against you. That last point matters if your rent occasionally runs a few days late.

Rental Kharma stands out by reporting your full payment history at your current address to two bureaus, offering unlimited credit mentoring, and backing the service with a 90-day money-back guarantee. The trade-off: your landlord must participate to verify payments each month.

RentReporters runs a four-step process. You sign up with basic information about yourself and your landlord, the company verifies up to four years of past payments, then reports to Equifax, TransUnion, and Experian. Covering all three bureaus matters when you don’t yet know which one a future lender will pull.

How do I actually enroll, step by step?

Enrollment is straightforward, but have a few documents ready before you start.

Step 1: Gather your documents. You’ll need your ITIN, your current lease agreement, and either your landlord’s contact information or access to the bank account you use to pay rent (for bank-linking services like Boom).

Step 2: Choose a service based on your situation. If your landlord won’t participate, go with a bank-linking platform. Boom is the main option here. If your landlord is cooperative and you want to report years of history at once, Rental Kharma or RentReporters are better fits.

Step 3: Sign up and enter your ITIN. Where the form asks for a Social Security Number or tax ID, enter your ITIN. The service routes that number to the bureau for file matching.

Step 4: Verify your rental history. For bank-linking services, you connect your checking account and the platform reads recurring rent transfers. For landlord-verification services, your landlord confirms the payment details. Either way, that data becomes part of your credit file once the bureau accepts it.

Step 5: Monitor your credit report. Check the bureaus the service reports to, typically within 30-45 days of enrollment. You can pull your reports for free at AnnualCreditReport.com using your ITIN. Our guide on how to check your credit score with an ITIN walks through that process in detail.

How much can rent reporting actually raise my ITIN credit score?

Readers frequently ask: results vary, but the numbers are real.

One provider claims its customers see an average increase of 28 points in the first two weeks after reporting their rent, with some reaching 105 points or more. Rental Kharma advertises an average 40-point increase, though individual results depend on what else is already on your credit profile.

For ITIN holders with a thin file, the impact tends to be larger. Adding any substantive positive tradeline moves a nearly empty file significantly. If you already have a secured card and a credit-builder loan reporting, rent adds a third data point to your payment history, which accounts for roughly 35% of your score under both FICO and VantageScore models. You can read more about how payment history drives your score in our payment history and ITIN credit score guide.

The benefit compounds over time. Once reported, your rent payment history appears as a tradeline month after month, as long as payments stay on time. Twelve months of on-time rent through a reporting service equals 12 months of positive payment history, the same credit-building effect as a credit-builder loan.

Can I report the rent I have already paid, going back years?

Yes, and this is one of the most underused features available to ITIN holders.

Retroactive reporting lets tenants add years of past payments to their credit file, depending on the service and how long they’ve been at their current address. Boom charges a one-time $25 fee to back-report up to 24 months. Rental Kharma reports all past rent at your current address for a flat rate, covering both TransUnion and Equifax, with no requirement to change your current payment arrangement with your landlord.

This is especially useful if you moved to the U.S. recently and have been renting for a year or two but haven’t established a U.S. credit file yet. Instead of starting your credit age at zero, you can plant months or years of positive payment history on your report immediately.

What if my landlord refuses to participate?

A question we hear often: it’s more common than you might expect, especially with private landlords or small property management companies.

Boom handles this directly. It reports to all three bureaus and lets users link their bank accounts to verify rent payments, so your landlord never needs to be involved. That makes it the most practical choice when your landlord is unresponsive or simply doesn’t want to participate.

For landlord-required services, the approach that tends to work is keeping the ask small. Point out that rent reporting may improve tenant retention, and that the landlord’s workload is usually just a monthly email confirmation. Some services handle all the reporting on the landlord’s behalf once they give initial consent.

If you pay through an online portal your landlord already uses, check whether it has a built-in reporting feature. Zillow Rental Payments, for example, lets tenants pay rent online, and if the landlord enrolls, Zillow reports each payment to Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion at no cost.

Does rent reporting work alongside other credit-building tools?

Yes, and combining them is the faster path.

Rent reporting adds one type of tradeline (a rental installment account), but it works best paired with other tools. A credit-builder loan with an ITIN adds a second account type and diversifies your credit mix. A secured card adds a revolving account, which FICO weights heavily when calculating utilization. Together, those three sources build a well-rounded credit file from almost nothing.

Payment history is the single largest factor in a FICO score. Rent reporting feeds directly into that factor, which is why it earns a place in any ITIN holder’s credit-building plan. If you want to see how all the pieces fit together, our guide on how to build credit with an ITIN number lays out a full sequence.
